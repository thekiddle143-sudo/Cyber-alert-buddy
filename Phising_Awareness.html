Phishing Awareness: Don't Take the Bait!

Learn to identify and avoid deceptive emails, messages, and websites designed to steal your information. **Duration: 8 minutes.**

Phishing: Cybercriminals' favorite bait.

What is Phishing? (1 min)

Phishing is a cybercrime where attackers try to trick you into revealing sensitive information (like usernames, passwords, credit card numbers) or installing malicious software. They do this by disguising themselves as a trustworthy entity in an electronic communication.

Think of it like this: A phishing email is like a criminal pretending to be your bank, asking for your account details. A phishing website is a fake website designed to look exactly like a legitimate one, so you type in your login credentials.

Module 1: Spotting the Red Flags in Emails & Messages (3 min)

Most phishing attempts have common signs. Always be suspicious of:

  • Urgency & Threats: "Your account will be suspended!" "Immediate action required!" Criminals use fear to make you act without thinking.
  • Generic Greetings: Instead of "Dear [Your Name]," it's "Dear Customer" or "Dear Account Holder." Legitimate organizations usually personalize emails.
  • Bad Grammar & Spelling: While not always present, errors are a strong indicator of a scam.
  • Suspicious Sender Address: The "From" name might say "Apple Support," but the actual email address is `apple-services@randomdomain.xyz`. Always check the full email address.
  • Unexpected Attachments: Never open attachments from unknown senders or unexpected ones, especially if they are Zip files or executables.

Self-Check:

"Did I expect this email/message? Does it feel *off* in any way?" Trust your gut feeling.

Module 2: Verifying Links & Websites (3 min)

The most common phishing tactic is getting you to click a malicious link. This link can lead to a fake login page or download malware.

  • Hover Before You Click: On a computer, hover your mouse over a link (don't click!) to see the actual URL in the bottom-left corner of your browser. Does it match the company's real website?
  • On Mobile: On touchscreens, often you can long-press a link to preview the URL.
  • Check for HTTPS: Always look for "https://" (the 's' is for secure) and a padlock icon in the address bar for sensitive sites (like banking or shopping).
  • Manually Type URLs: If an email asks you to log in to a service, open your browser and *type the address yourself* (e.g., `www.yourbank.com`) instead of clicking a link.
  • Look for subtle misspellings: `amazon.co.uk` vs `amaz0n.co.uk` (zero instead of o).

What to Do if You Spot Phishing:

  • **Do NOT click** any links or open attachments.
  • **Do NOT reply.**
  • **Delete** the email/message.
  • **Report** it if your email provider offers a "Report Phishing" option.
